# RESEARCH PROPOSAL

## Title: **IntrospectBench**: A Cross-Domain Benchmark for Evaluating Step-Level Reasoning Introspection in Large Language Models

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Context and Motivation

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive reasoning capabilities when generating chain-of-thought (CoT) solutions. However, recent studies reveal a critical limitation: LLMs struggle to reliably detect errors in their own reasoning steps. Huang et al. (2024) demonstrated that LLMs "cannot self-correct reasoning yet" without external feedback, while Kamoi et al. (2024) found that top models like GPT-4 have low recall in detecting reasoning errors. This gap between generation and verification capability poses a fundamental challenge for trustworthy deployment.

Current benchmarks primarily evaluate final-answer accuracy or self-correction with external feedback. However, the intrinsic ability of LLMs to introspectively identify errors in their reasoning steps—their *reasoning introspection* capability—remains under-evaluated. This capability is crucial for:
- **Self-correction without oracles**: Detecting errors before finalizing answers
- **Test-time compute scaling**: Knowing when to continue reasoning vs. terminate
- **Trustworthy deployment**: Providing confidence estimates about reasoning quality

### 1.2 Problem Statement

Existing benchmarks exhibit three key limitations:

1. **Domain specificity**: MWP-MISTAKE (Singh et al., ACL 2025) focuses exclusively on mathematical reasoning. PRISM-Bench (Qian et al., 2025) covers only visual puzzles. There is no systematic evaluation across reasoning domains.

2. **Task scope**: CorrectBench (Tie et al., 2025) evaluates self-correction *methods* but does not isolate the fundamental capability of step-level error detection. ProcessBench (Zheng et al., 2024) targets process reward models rather than LLM introspection.

3. **Evaluation granularity**: Most benchmarks provide only aggregate accuracy metrics. They do not distinguish between (a) detecting that an error exists, (b) localizing which step contains the error, and (c) understanding the error type.

### 1.3 Key Insight and Hypothesis

**Key Insight**: Human reasoning is characterized by the ability to monitor and evaluate one's own thought processes—metacognitive monitoring (Flavell, 1979; Brown, 1987). Current LLMs generate reasoning traces but lack reliable mechanisms for introspective evaluation. A systematic benchmark for this capability would enable targeted improvements.

**Hypothesis**: LLMs will exhibit domain-dependent reasoning introspection capabilities, with stronger error detection in structured domains (mathematics, code) compared to open-ended domains (commonsense reasoning). Furthermore, error detection performance will degrade as error position moves deeper in reasoning chains.

### 1.4 Research Questions

1. **RQ1 (Detection)**: How accurately can LLMs detect the presence of errors in multi-step reasoning chains across different domains?

2. **RQ2 (Localization)**: Can LLMs identify the specific step where an error occurs, and how does localization accuracy vary by error position and domain?

3. **RQ3 (Characterization)**: Can LLMs classify the type of reasoning error (e.g., calculation, logic, factuality), and does this capability transfer across domains?

4. **RQ4 (Self vs. Other)**: Is error detection more accurate for self-generated reasoning versus reasoning generated by other models?

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Self-Correction and Self-Evaluation in LLMs

The ability of LLMs to self-correct has been extensively studied with mixed results. Madaan et al. (2023) proposed Self-Refine for iterative refinement, while Shinn et al. (2023) introduced Reflexion with verbal reinforcement signals. However, Huang et al. (2024) provided empirical evidence that LLMs struggle with *intrinsic* self-correction—correction without external feedback or ground-truth labels.

Recent work has explored confidence estimation as a proxy for self-evaluation. Mavi et al. (2025) demonstrated that stepwise evaluation outperforms holistic scoring for failure detection. Ghasemabadi & Niu (2025) proposed Gnosis, which decodes correctness signals from hidden states with ~5M parameters, achieving strong performance without external judges.

### 2.2 Benchmarks for Reasoning Evaluation

**Math-focused benchmarks**: MWP-MISTAKE (Singh et al., ACL 2025) introduces 1,500+ math problems with both correct and incorrect reasoning steps generated via rule-based perturbations. Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2023) and PRM800K (Lightman et al., 2023) provide step-level annotations for process supervision.

**Multi-domain benchmarks**: CorrectBench (Tie et al., 2025) evaluates self-correction across commonsense, mathematical, and code reasoning. However, it evaluates end-to-end self-correction rather than step-level introspection.

**Non-monotonic reasoning benchmarks**: Belief-R (Wilie et al., EMNLP 2024) evaluates belief revision in LLMs with ~1,744 synthetic logical inference problems using modus ponens/tollens. LogicBench (Parmar et al., ACL 2024) includes non-monotonic reasoning as one of 25 logical patterns. MultiLogicNMR(er) (Xiu & Liu, EMNLP 2025) extends this to multi-extension non-monotonic reasoning. These works focus on logical belief update scenarios, while IntrospectBench focuses on step-level error detection in natural reasoning chains.

**Process-level evaluation**: ProcessBench (Zheng et al., 2024) and MPBench (Ai et al., ACL 2025 Findings) focus on process reward model evaluation. CriticBench (Lin et al., 2024) benchmarks critique-correct reasoning but does not isolate error detection.

### 2.3 Metacognitive Approaches

Think² (Elenjical et al., 2026) operationalizes Ann Brown's regulatory cycle (Planning, Monitoring, Evaluation) as a prompting framework, demonstrating improved error diagnosis. Metacognitive Prompting (Wang & Zhao, 2024) introduces a five-stage reflective process but evaluates primarily on NLU benchmarks that Zhang et al. (2025) showed skew toward low-level cognitive skills.

### 2.4 How This Work Differs

| Aspect | Existing Work | **IntrospectBench** (This Work) |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| **Primary focus** | Self-correction outcomes | Step-level introspection capability |
| **Domains** | Math-only or general accuracy | Math, Logic, Commonsense, Code |
| **Error control** | Natural errors or SLM-generated | Controlled injection at known positions |
| **Metrics** | Final answer accuracy | Detection, Localization, Classification |
| **Evaluation target** | End-to-end systems | Intrinsic LLM capabilities |

**Key distinction**: While MWP-MISTAKE focuses on mathematical mistakes, CorrectBench evaluates self-correction methods, and Belief-R tests belief revision with synthetic logical inferences (ΔR framework with update/maintain scenarios), IntrospectBench systematically evaluates the foundational capability of *reasoning introspection*—detecting, localizing, and characterizing errors in natural language CoT reasoning across multiple domains with dedicated metrics for each sub-capability.

## 3. Proposed Approach

### 3.1 Benchmark Overview

**IntrospectBench** is a multi-domain benchmark for evaluating step-level reasoning introspection in LLMs. The benchmark contains **1,200 problems** across four reasoning domains:

| Domain | Source Datasets | Problems | Avg Steps |
|--------|----------------|----------|-----------|
| **Mathematical** | GSM8K, MATH-500 | 400 | 5-8 |
| **Logical** | LogiQA, ProofWriter | 300 | 4-7 |
| **Commonsense** | CommonsenseQA, StrategyQA | 300 | 3-6 |
| **Code** | MBPP, HumanEval | 200 | 4-8 |

### 3.2 Data Construction Pipeline

#### Step 1: Collect Base Problems
We sample problems from existing datasets, filtering for those requiring explicit multi-step reasoning. We generate ground-truth CoT solutions using GPT-4o with verification.

#### Step 2: Error Injection
For each problem, we create corrupted CoT variants by injecting controlled errors at specific steps:

**Error taxonomy** (6 types):
1. **Calculation error**: Arithmetic mistakes, algebraic manipulation errors
2. **Logic error**: Invalid deductive steps, fallacies, incorrect implications
3. **Factuality error**: Incorrect factual statements, definition misuse
4. **Omission error**: Missing critical reasoning steps
5. **Misinterpretation error**: Incorrect understanding of problem constraints
6. **Premature conclusion**: Drawing conclusions without sufficient evidence

**Error positions**: Early (step 1-2), Middle (step 3-4), Late (step 5+)

**Injection method**: Human annotators and rule-based transformations create errors that:
- Are semantically coherent (plausible but incorrect)
- Have unambiguous error locations
- Represent realistic mistake patterns

#### Step 3: Quality Verification
Each corrupted CoT is verified by two independent annotators to ensure:
- Exactly one error exists at the designated position
- The error is detectable from the reasoning context
- Earlier steps are correct and would lead to correct conclusions if followed

### 3.3 Evaluation Tasks

IntrospectBench evaluates three progressively more difficult aspects of reasoning introspection:

#### Task 1: Error Detection (Binary Classification)
Given a problem and CoT, determine whether the reasoning contains any errors.
- **Metric**: Accuracy, F1-score, AUC-ROC
- **Baseline**: Random (0.5), Always-correct heuristic

#### Task 2: Error Localization (Step Identification)
Given a problem and CoT known to contain an error, identify the step number containing the first error.
- **Metric**: Exact match accuracy, Mean Absolute Error in step position, within-1 accuracy
- **Baseline**: Random guessing, always-predict-middle

#### Task 3: Error Characterization (Type Classification)
Given a problem and CoT with a known error at a specific step, classify the error type from the 6-type taxonomy.
- **Metric**: Accuracy, Confusion matrix, Per-type F1
- **Baseline**: Majority class, uniform random

### 3.4 Novel Evaluation Metrics

Beyond standard accuracy, we introduce:

1. **Introspection Score (IS)**: A composite metric combining detection, localization, and characterization:
   $$IS = \frac{1}{3}(Acc_{detect} + Acc_{localize} + Acc_{characterize})$$

2. **Position-Weighted Localization Score**: Localization accuracy weighted by error position (earlier errors are easier):
   $$PWLS = \frac{\sum_{i} w_i \cdot \mathbb{1}[\hat{s}_i = s_i]}{\sum_i w_i}$$
   where $w_i = 1/\sqrt{s_i}$ and $s_i$ is the true error step.

3. **Domain Calibration Score**: Measures consistency of introspection capability across domains:
   $$DCS = 1 - \frac{\sigma_{domain}}{\mu_{domain}}$$
   where $\sigma$ and $\mu$ are computed across domain-specific IS scores.

4. **Self-Other Gap**: Difference in performance on self-generated vs. other-model-generated reasoning.

## 4. Experimental Plan

### 4.1 Models Evaluated

We evaluate a diverse set of models across size and type:

| Model | Size | Type | Access |
|-------|------|------|--------|
| GPT-4o | Unknown | Proprietary (frontier) | API |
| GPT-4o-mini | Unknown | Proprietary (efficient) | API |
| Llama-3.1 | 8B, 70B | Open-weight | Local |
| Qwen-2.5 | 7B, 32B, 72B | Open-weight (reasoning) | Local |
| DeepSeek-R1-Distill | 7B, 14B, 32B | Reasoning-specialized | Local |

### 4.2 Experimental Protocol

**Phase 1: Baseline Evaluation**
- Evaluate all models on all three tasks across all domains
- Compare prompting strategies: direct, CoT-verification, metacognitive prompting
- Establish baseline performance distributions

**Phase 2: Ablation Studies**
- Vary error position to study depth sensitivity
- Compare performance on self-generated vs. externally-generated CoTs
- Test cross-domain transfer: train on math, test on logic

**Phase 3: Analysis**
- Correlate introspection scores with base reasoning ability
- Analyze error type sensitivity (which error types are hardest to detect)
- Investigate confidence calibration: do models know when they're uncertain?

### 4.3 Expected Results

**H1**: Error detection accuracy will be highest for Mathematical reasoning (structured, verifiable) and lowest for Commonsense reasoning (open-ended, ambiguous).

**H2**: Localization accuracy will decrease as error position moves deeper in reasoning chains due to error propagation and context accumulation.

**H3**: Reasoning-specialized models (DeepSeek-R1, Qwen-2.5 with thinking) will outperform generalist models on introspection tasks, but the gap will be smaller than for generation tasks.

**H4**: Models will exhibit an "introspection gap"—they will be better at detecting errors in other models' reasoning than in their own (confirmation bias).

## 5. Success Criteria

### 5.1 Confirming Evidence

The hypothesis would be supported by:
1. Significant domain-dependent variation in introspection scores (>15% gap between best/worst domains)
2. Monotonic decrease in localization accuracy with error depth
3. Error type sensitivity: calculation errors detected more reliably than logic errors
4. Strong correlation (r > 0.7) between detection and localization performance

### 5.2 Refuting Evidence

The hypothesis would be challenged by:
1. Uniform performance across all domains (suggesting domain-agnostic introspection)
2. Flat localization accuracy across error positions
3. No difference between self-generated and other-generated reasoning detection

### 5.3 Contribution Assessment

This work makes the following contributions:

1. **Cross-domain benchmark for reasoning introspection**: While existing benchmarks focus on specific domains (MWP-MISTAKE for math, Belief-R for logical belief revision), IntrospectBench is the first to systematically evaluate step-level reasoning introspection across mathematical, logical, commonsense, and code domains with dedicated metrics for detection, localization, and characterization.

2. **Controlled error injection methodology**: Systematic evaluation requires knowing ground-truth error locations. Our annotation protocol ensures unambiguous error positions.

3. **Fine-grained evaluation framework**: Beyond accuracy, we measure detection, localization, and characterization as distinct capabilities with different cognitive demands.

4. **Empirical characterization of introspection capabilities**: We provide systematic evidence for how well current LLMs can introspectively evaluate their reasoning, with actionable insights for improvement.

## 8. Limitations and Future Work

### 8.1 Limitations

1. **Synthetic errors**: While controlled, injected errors may not perfectly represent natural model errors.
2. **English-only**: Initial release focuses on English reasoning; extension to multilingual reasoning is future work.
3. **Static benchmark**: Does not evaluate interactive introspection (asking clarifying questions).

### 8.2 Future Directions

1. **Dynamic error injection**: Use adversarial methods to generate harder-to-detect errors based on model weaknesses.
2. **Interactive introspection**: Evaluate whether models can ask clarifying questions to resolve reasoning uncertainty.
3. **Training for introspection**: Use IntrospectBench to train and fine-tune models for improved reasoning self-monitoring.
